Introduction

This project examines how Nazi Germany used mass media to promote its ideology of racial superiority and to make extremist policies appear normal, scientific, and necessary. Posters, newspapers, radio broadcasts, and speeches shaped what people believed about race, community, and loyalty.

Led by Joseph Goebbels, the Reich Ministry of Public Enlightenment and Propaganda controlled nearly every form of communication in Germany. By eliminating alternative viewpoints and saturating public life with Nazi messaging, the regime created an environment where extremist ideology appeared to be common sense.

The Role of the Ministry

Through newspapers, radio, schoolbooks, films, and public posters, the Propaganda Ministry shaped expectations about who belonged in German society and who did not.
